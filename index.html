MANHATTAN NURSING AIDE CAREER CENTER

This hypothetical charity is modeled after Robin Hood’s Job Training and Economic Security portfolio.

The Manhattan Nursing Aide Career Center (MNACC) provides training and job placement services to New York’s un- and under-employed, helping them find work as a nursing aide. This career track is a promising route out of poverty for a variety of reasons: it pays around $9.50 an hour, has reasonable education requirements, and is expected to grow as Baby Boomers keep retiring.

Another advantage for MNACC is that trainees are primarily women and may not speak English as a primary language, suggesting they would otherwise be disadvantaged in the job market.

Here, the grant under consideration will pay for additional instructors and support staff, letting the Career Center handle additional trainees.

IMPACT

Robin Hood identifies the specific ways each charity is improving the well-being of New York’s poor. We then use our set of metrics research to estimate how each contributes to New Yorkers' quality of life. Here are the metrics we'd use for MNACC:

  1. Job training and placement: Impact of initial wage for full-time employees (grantee reported pre-training wage data)

    Impact = P.D.V. of ( A * B% * [ $C - $D ] )

    A: number of program participants
    B: percent of participants that graduate the program and remain employed for one year, solely because of the program
    C: average annual post-training earnings
    D: average annual pre-training earnings

  2. Job training and placement: Impact of future wage increases over and above wage of initial placement

    Impact = P.D.V. of ( A * B% * [ $C - $D ] )

    A: number of program participants
    B: percent of participants that graduate the program and remain employed for one year, solely because of the program
    C: average annual post-training earnings
    D: average annual pre-training earnings

RESEARCH

All of these numbers have to come from somewhere. At Robin Hood our staff use the most recent social science research to better understand the true impact of our interventions, like the lifelong effects of ensuring a student graduates from school, or the importance of finding and stopping domestic abuse early. This knowledge then informs the values that go into our metrics, to ensure we’re always making as accurate decisions as possible.
